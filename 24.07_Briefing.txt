[2] SO LIEF DIE DEBATTE UM DIE GUTERRES-NACHFOLGE

Wer folgt auf UN-Generalsekretär Guterres? Sechs Bewerberinnen und Bewerber stellten sich in einer öffentlichen Debatte vor. Was sie einte: der Wunsch nach starken Vereinten Nationen.

Der große Verlierer der Debatte war im Saal der Generalversammlung gar nicht anwesend. Der noch amtierende UN-Generalsekretär António Guterres wurde zwar namentlich nicht erwähnt, doch alle Kandidaten für seine Nachfolge kamen zu einem ähnlich ernüchternden Urteil: Die Vereinten Nationen stehen derzeit im Abseits.

Auf den Punkt brachte es der Argentinier Rafael Grossi, der Direktor der Internationalen Atomenergie-Organisation IAEA:

Wobei Guterres wahrscheinlich entgegnet hätte, dass dies nicht seine Schuld sei, sondern die der Vetomächte im UN-Sicherheitsrat, die sich bei den wichtigsten Kriegen und Konflikten oft gegenseitig blockieren.

Dass ein Generalsekretär oft mehr "Sekretär" als "General" ist, wollte Grossi nicht gelten lassen. Der 65-Jährige sagte, in seinem jetzigen Amt als IAEA-Chef habe er mehrfach erfolgreich Russland und die Ukraine zusammengebracht: "Wir haben bis jetzt sechs Feuerpausen vermittelt, um unverzichtbare Reparaturen am Atomkraftwerk Saporischschja durchzuführen, die einen großen Unfall verhindert haben."

Für die Vereinten Nationen geht ein Jahr unter erschwerten Bedingungen zu Ende.

Unter den vier Kandidatinnen hinterließ Rebeca Grynspan den besten Eindruck. Die ehemalige Vizepräsidentin von Costa Rica ist Wirtschaftsexpertin und Generalsekretärin der UN-Konferenz für Handel und Entwicklung (UNCTAD). Auch sie habe erfolgreich zwischen Russland und der Ukraine vermittelt, betonte Grynspan.

Vor vier Jahren erreichte sie das Getreideabkommen, das eine weltweite Hungerkrise verhinderte. Als UN-Generalsekretärin werde sie sich für Frieden durch gemeinsamen Handel einsetzen und gegen Zölle und Protektionismus. Nach ihrer ersten fünfjährigen Amtszeit solle niemand mehr fragen: "Wo ist die UN?" Stattdessen wünsche sie eine UN, die "wieder mit am Tisch sitzt".

Die Fragen der beiden Moderatoren und der im Saal anwesenden UN-Botschafter waren breit gestreut: Was ist zu tun, wenn eine Vetomacht blockiert? Was ist in den ersten 100 Tagen in Angriff zu nehmen? Wie kann die UN reformiert und effizienter gemacht machen?

Ende 2026 läuft die Amtszeit von UN-Generalsekretär Guterres aus - wer folgt auf ihn?

Eher blass und zu umständlich in ihren Antworten blieb die bekannteste Bewerberin: Michelle Bachelet, die frühere Präsidentin von Chile. Ihre Kandidatur gilt schon jetzt als aussichtslos, weil ihr Einsatz für Frauenrechte der Trump-Regierung missfällt. Auch die frühere Außenministerin von Ecuador, María Fernanda Espinosa, und der frühere Präsident von Senegal, Macky Sall, konnten nicht überzeugen.

Bei den anwesenden UN-Mitarbeitern im Saal kam die jüngste Kandidatin am besten an: die 52-jährige UN-Botschafterin aus Guyana, Carolyn Rodrigues-Birkett.

Am Ende ausschlaggebend sind jedoch die 15 Mitgliedsstaaten im UN-Sicherheitsrat. Erforderlich sind dort mindestens neun Ja-Stimmen. Und wichtiger noch: Keine der fünf Vetomächte darf Nein sagen. Gut möglich, dass am Ende die USA, China und Russland die Guterres-Nachfolge doch wieder unter sich ausmachen.

Ebenfalls möglich ist, dass der Wunsch von Annalena Baerbock, Präsidentin der UN-Generalversammlung, nach der ersten Frau an der Spitze der Vereinten Nationen wieder nicht in Erfüllung geht.

[1] AN FDA PANEL JUST ENDORSED THESE UNPROVEN PEPTIDES

A panel of experts on Thursday narrowly recommended that the Food and Drug Administration reclassify four popular peptides—including one endorsed by podcaster Joe Rogan—to allow certain pharmacies to legally dispense them.

In an at-times emotionally charged meeting that lasted more than 11 hours, members of an FDA advisory committee heard data presentations from FDA staff on four unapproved peptides—BPC-157, KPV, TB-500, and MOTs-C—as well as testimony from health care practitioners, patients, pharmacies, and clinics.

Members voted eight to six, with one abstention, to add three of the four peptides to the agency’s bulks list, meaning compounding pharmacies can use them to create custom medications for patients. (The fourth peptide—MOTs-C—received a seven-to-five vote in favor, with two abstentions.) The advisory panel, which includes several members tied to peptide-related businesses and telehealth clinics, will consider three more peptides on Friday.

Peptide therapies have been growing in popularity thanks to the endorsement of podcasters, wellness influencers, and Health and Human Services secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., who has said he’s a “big fan.” Essentially smaller versions of proteins, peptides are being touted for everything from skin rejuvenation and hair growth to gut health and injury recovery despite a lack of scientific evidence to back up these claims.

Many peptides currently exist in an unregulated gray market after the Biden administration banned compounding pharmacies from producing 20 peptides out of safety concerns in 2023. They can be legally sold online for “research use only” and “not for human consumption,” yet their marketing heavily implies otherwise. China and India are major suppliers of peptides, with some crypto-funded fentanyl labs switching gears to produce them.

During the meeting, the FDA’s own scientists recommended against allowing compounding pharmacies to produce any of the seven peptides being considered due to a lack of evidence showing they are safe and effective. Peptide proponents meanwhile testified that US compounding pharmacies should be able to produce them so that patients can access safer, regulated options with a prescription.

“I'm not going to tell you that the evidence for BPC-157 is strong. It isn't, and I won't overclaim it," said Anant Vinjamoori, chief medical officer of telehealth provider Hims & Hers. “What I'd ask is that you not read thin as non-existent.” The company is actively preparing to launch peptide treatments in anticipation of the FDA greenlighting some peptides.

Among the peptides approved, two are particularly popular with podcasters and influencers. BPC-157 and TB-500 are paired as part of a so-called “Wolverine stack” to help improve recovery after injuries. Committee members were asked to evaluate BPC-157, which is thought to promote tissue repair, for ulcerative colitis. The World Anti-Doping Agency says BPC-157 poses a risk to athlete health because it has not been studied extensively in humans.

Some health care providers testified that patients of theirs had turned to the gray market to buy peptides and ended up with products that were laced with illicit substances. But while FDA approval may help reduce some of those risks, other commenters said it could create new ones.

John Hertig, chair of the Collaborative for Evidence-Based Medicines, said allowing compounders to make these peptides sends the message that the FDA is approving them. “That gets very confusing for our patients,” he said, adding that the existence of a gray market isn’t a reason to allow compounders to make these peptides.

He argued that there will still be a gray market even if the FDA allows for compounding of these peptides, citing that patients still seek out gray market GLP-1 drugs despite several FDA-approved drugs on the market.

Adriane Fugh-Berman, a physician and professor at Georgetown University, also challenged the idea that allowing legal versions would eliminate this gray market. “Would the government endorse companies manufacturing heroin because it would be cleaner than what's sold on the street?” she said. “Just because the market is large, doesn’t mean it should be legalized. It means that a product should be studied.”

The experts who voted against adding these four peptides to the bulks list cited a lack of data to support their use.

Elizabeth Rebello, an anesthesiologist at the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center, was one of the committee members who voted against allowing the four peptides to be compounded, citing unknowns around safety and confusion for the public.

“I'm just concerned that we're entering a Pandora's box here,” she said.

For now, the panel’s recommendations are just that. A formal FDA decision will likely not come for several months.

[2] META’S NEW FEEL-GOOD AI AD USES A SONG ABOUT THE WORLD ENDING

On Thursday morning, Meta published an ad to its official Instagram account with the message that artificial intelligence will not “make us less connected” or “leave us behind.” It was set to the 1972 David Bowie track “Five Years,” which is about, among other things, the end of the world.

“Call us optimists, call us dreamers, call us whatever the hell you want—but we’re betting on people, and we like those odds,” the narrator says, as a toddler plays with a butterfly, two parents laugh with their infant, and Jalen Brunson waves at the Knicks Championship Parade. (The ad also takes special care to highlight use cases for its controversial smart glasses that are not, say, harassing women while surreptitiously recording them.)

The snippet of “Five Years” used in the ad includes the following lyrics:

My brain hurt like a warehouse, it had no room to spare

I had to cram so many things to store everything in there

Perhaps understandably, it skips other parts of the song, including the opening:

News had just come overWe had five years left to cry inNews guy wept and told usEarth was really dying

When reached for comment, Meta spokesperson Francis Brennan said he “would like to highlight what David Bowie himself said about ‘Five Years’ and its meaning: having optimism for the future.” Brennan sent along a 1972 quote about the song that he sourced to BowieBible.com.

“People are so incredibly serious and scared of the future that I would wish to turn the feeling the other way, into a wave of optimism,” reads the quote, which is itself sourced from NME Magazine. “If one can take the micky out of the future, and what it is going to be like … It’s going to be unbelievably technological.”

Nevertheless, BowieBible.com also notes that the song “speaks of a dystopian nightmare in which Earth enters its final five years, for reasons undisclosed, and the ensuing panic, violence, and attempts at redemption.”

BowieBible.com also quotes from a 2018 interview with drummer Mick Woodmansey: “Well, we chatted about it, and we knew it was about the end of the world and pretty depressing,” Woodmansey said of the song. “I remember when we recorded it, he was actually in tears. He was doing the vocal and he was actually crying.”

According to Jeremy D. Larson, the deputy director of Condé Nast sister publication Pitchfork, taking songs out of context for commercials is a time-honored tradition. He points to a Carnival Cruise ad that uses an Iggy Pop song about heroin addiction. (He also says that since Warner Chappell Music owns Bowie’s catalog, they are the ones with discretion over where his songs end up.)

“Meta choosing this specific song where the context for human connection is the apocalypse seems either really ignorant or really insidious,” Larson tells WIRED over Slack. “I don't know, maybe in that context it's genius.”

“Hearing [Bowie’s] work used for AI just feels especially and specifically nonconsensual,” he adds. (CEO Mark Zuckerberg, in a post about the ad, wrote that the company was “focused on giving every person the tools to reach your full potential and making sure the benefits of technology are distributed to everyone.”)

Over the past few years, tech companies have made several heavy-handed attempts to assuage people’s fears about their technology making the world worse. Just a few weeks ago, Anthropic released its own lofty ad, “There’s hope in hard questions.” It opens with a clip of a burning building and asks, “Who’s gonna hit the brakes if we need to?” before flashing over dozens of graves. The tone of the ad gradually shifts toward optimism, and ends with vague questions like, “What if we started to be more human again?” over images of families and a humpback whale.

In 2024, Google released a baffling ad about a father teaching his daughter, an aspiring athlete, how to hurdle. The father decides that he should use Gemini to draft a letter, from the perspective of his daughter, so that she can send it to decorated hurdler Sydney McLaughlin-Levrone. And during the 2022 Super Bowl, FTX aired a now-notorious ad starring Larry David, playing various Larry-esque characters, dismissing useful inventions throughout history—the toilet! the fork! the lightbulb!—before ultimately dismissing FTX.

“Don't be like Larry,” reads the text overlay at the end of the ad. “Don't miss out on crypto, on NFTs, on the next big thing.”

Of course, it’s understandable that tech companies would use every tool at their disposal to increase goodwill. But the ads also demonstrate that many tech companies are failing to sell products that independently convince people that they are not actively making the world worse.

[3] FOR TAYLOR SWIFT, MADISON SQUARE GARDEN’S CONTROVERSIAL CAMERAS BRIEFLY WENT DARK

Madison Square Garden owner James Dolan has insisted that the face-recognition system and array of surveillance cameras deployed at his venues is “very, very useful for security”—a big reason why MSG “is the most secure venue in the country.”

On the night of July 2, security in and around the Garden was particularly intense. Roughly 130 cops were deployed to the area. New York City officials were preparing to shut down the surrounding blocks to both vehicles and pedestrians the next day for a major, major event.

July 2 was, of course, the kickoff to Taylor Swift and Travis Kelce’s wedding celebration, an approximately 100-person rehearsal dinner at Madison Square Garden’s Infosys Theater. And yet, on that extremely sensitive evening, Dolan’s MSG locked nearly all of its security team out of the network of surveillance cameras inside the arena, according to an internal document reviewed by WIRED. The move functionally prevented MSG security from fully operating the venue’s controversial face-recognition program that evening, two sources familiar with the Garden’s operations say.

While the shutdown order reviewed by WIRED applied only to July 2, those sources add that they were told the lockout was repeated on July 3, Swift and Kelce’s wedding night, which drew roughly 1,000 of the biggest names in media, music, business, and sports.

At every stage of their planning, Swift’s and Kelce’s teams went to extraordinary lengths to keep private what happened inside the Garden. Guests had to sign a nondisclosure agreement before they could receive a digital invitation. Tents were set up for cars to park in, so people could move in and out in relative secrecy (and, presumably, avoid the biometric surveillance gear positioned at the standard entrances). Wedding attendees were told there was a no-phone policy. The only detailed description of the event posted by a wedding night guest was quickly deleted.

According to the internal document, an MSG executive ordered on July 2 a seven-hour lockdown of the Garden’s video management system, made by the security tech company Genetec, “due to a client privacy request.” Security staffers who ordinarily monitor the building’s internal cameras and the accompanying face-recognition software were barred from doing so. The memo states that only a handful of company leaders and engineers were allowed into their Genetec accounts. The rest were “disabled.”

“If you somehow still have access to cameras after 3PM, you will be forcibly logged off,” the document warned MSG security personnel.

Since 2018, Dolan’s company has experimented with biometric surveillance at its venues, which include Radio City Music Hall and the Beacon Theater in New York, as well as the Sphere in Las Vegas. The organization contends that the technology is a security measure, but there are many publicly documented cases where the face-recognition system appears to have been for something else: retribution.

Fans who’ve been critical of Dolan have had their faces captured and then put on watch lists. A lawyer representing a cop was banned in April because his client is suing MSG. A mom trying to take her then 9-year-old Girl Scout to a Christmas show at Radio City Music Hall had her face flagged and was blocked from entering. The reason: She was an attorney, and her firm was in a legal dispute with one Dolan-controlled entity.

Genetec’s software serves as the backbone for MSG’s video security system, monitoring feeds from the various cameras and allowing security personnel to manage the applications that rely on those feeds, including face recognition. Cameras at the guest entrances are tied into a face-recognition system made by eConnect. If someone’s face triggers an alert, Genetec then allows that person to be easily tracked across the camera network. According to a person familiar with the security surveillance setup, “This makes it much easier to track a person, because we can jump from camera to camera in real time and follow them.”

As WIRED previously reported, Garden security used these systems to monitor a transgender woman second-by-second when she was in the arena. The cameras even noted when she went in and out of a bathroom.

Attendees at the Swift-Kelce pre-wedding celebration were spared such prying eyes. Civil libertarians say they understand why the couple might not want Dolan’s surveillance system deployed in full. But those activists see the accommodation as more than a one-off. It’s a preview of “a future where privacy is a luxury for the elite few, and the rest of us are subjected to pervasive surveillance everywhere we go,” says Evan Greer, director of the digital rights group Fight for the Future.

MSG’s venues host private events not infrequently. NBCUniversal takes over Radio City Music Hall every year to preview its shows for the next season; Warner Bros. Discovery goes to other Dolan-owned properties. But those customers did not ask to disable any aspect of the Garden’s security system, according to a source with direct knowledge of the matter. “It has never been done,” the source says, reflecting on their years at MSG, “and the request wouldn’t have been entertained.”

Even when the New York Police Department takes over Madison Square Garden, the cameras have stayed on. The NYPD regularly holds its graduation ceremonies from the Police Academy within the arena. As WIRED previously reported, the MSG face-recognition system contained a photograph apparently taken from one of those events. It shows an officer in his Police Academy dress uniform.

There’s the argument that Swift and Kelce, two of the planet’s most famous people, bought some modicum of privacy for themselves and their guests when they spent the estimated $1.2 to $1.6 million to book the Garden. It’s a venue with “some unique advantages,” former NYPD intelligence official John Miller noted for CNN. “The Garden has no windows, is covered by a roof, and access to the venue can be tightly controlled. That limits the ability of paparazzi, helicopters with powerful, long-distance cameras, or drones to capture the kinds of images they have at other celebrity weddings in outdoor venues.” Of course, for that money, they could have reserved any number of islands more private than Manhattan and an event space more discreet than the so-called “World’s Most Famous Arena.”

“People who have their wedding at Madison Square Garden don’t make that choice for privacy,” says a security professional who is deeply familiar with the venue.

Early July was a particularly hectic time. The city was considered to be in a “heightened threat environment” by the New York Police Department because of the America 250 celebrations and ongoing war with Iran. (That threat estimate is presumably one reason why the cameras outside the Garden—which can be accessed by the NYPD—stayed on, according to the memo.) Swift’s presence, of course, amped up the stakes enormously. In May, a man was sentenced to 15 years in prison for planning an attack at her concert in Vienna, Austria.

The Garden’s interior camera network is supposed to help in those situations, and more. What if someone snuck into the ultra-exclusive event? What if that person was a real threat to the people inside? Dolan, in a 2023 interview, told a local news anchor that the combination of surveillance cameras and face recognition did important work to keep such dangerous people out: “Are you someone who is on this list? If you’re a terrorist it’ll say, ‘That’s a terrorist.’”

But the NYPD supplies no such data to the Garden, a department spokesperson previously told WIRED. While some photographs from the FBI’s most-wanted list have been uploaded into the system, a lawsuit from a former MSG security staffer contends that was done to establish “an after-the-fact false narrative” that the face recognition was primarily geared toward stopping hostile actors rather than individuals Dolan perceived to be personal threats.

“Clearly the technology is not that helpful if it wasn’t needed at one of MSG’s highest-profile events of the year,” Michelle Dahl, executive director for the Surveillance Technology Oversight Project, said in a statement.

That said, there are indications that some cameras were rolling during the event. A notice near the Garden’s entrance alerted visitors that there would be “photography and videography” on the premises from June 29 to July 3—the date of the wedding—and that, by entering, guests agreed that their images and voices could be used in “any and all manner, format, and media.”

The Garden has long had a reputation for being the epicenter of global entertainment, a place that attracts the very brightest stars. But the Garden’s handling of its high-profile visitors has come under scrutiny recently, after the hacker collective ShinyHunters posted an internal MSG database of more than 39,000 VIPs. MSG sued WIRED for defamation for its reporting on the database; in the lawsuit, MSG confirmed that the “spreadsheet-like export” was stolen after an employee’s credentials were compromised by a social-engineering attack and that it no longer employed that individual. The lawsuit also confirmed that a small subset of those celebrities were designated some sort of “risk.” Among the 400 or so found by WIRED are the actor and musician Jamie Foxx; former “Real Housewife” Ubah Hassan; Shang-Chi star Simu Liu; Formula One driver Lewis Hamilton; and Achraf Hakimi, the captain of Morocco’s World Cup team.

Sources previously indicated to WIRED that these risk scores could reflect internal assessments of whether the VIPs might say something less than positive about Dolan and the Garden’s management or become involved, even indirectly, with some sort of public relations issue. MSG acknowledged that it maintains a “Threat Management notes field” for VIPs that might address, among other things, “prior incidents at MSG venues, the conduct or intensity of an individual’s fanbase or associates, or identity-verification concerns.” There was “a separate database” for “genuine security risks,” the lawsuit noted.

The celebrity database also included fields for race, gender, and sexual orientation, as MSG confirmed in its suit. The actor Elliot Page and the beauty influencer Emira D’Spain, for instance, were two of the 93 VIPs marked as “LGBTQIA,” as were the singer Avery Wilson and the designer Zac Posen.

“James Dolan has serious questions to answer. First, why is he so obsessed with us?” Tony Simone, an openly gay New York state lawmaker whose district includes midtown Manhattan, posted on July 9.

The company, for its part, states that its “support for the LGBTQIA community is indisputable.” The sexual orientation field, MSG added in its lawsuit against WIRED, was used for “inclusion-oriented outreach, including Pride events, community engagement, sales opportunities, and donations.”

Page, D’Spain, Posen, and Wilson also received a “risk” designation in the Garden database, albeit a “low” one. (In its lawsuit, MSG notes that only 4 percent of the celebrities it “tagged with an orientation of ‘LGBTQIA’” have threat-management notes.) Some of Swift and Kelce’s guests were also given risk scores, including Selena Gomez; her husband, the producer Benny Blanco; the broadcaster and former football player Michael Strahan; the actress Mariska Hargitay; the singer Benson Boone; and the rapper Ice Spice.

Representatives for Swift and Kelce did not respond to requests to comment for this story. In an email, a spokesperson for MSG declined to comment “due to the fact that we are in active litigation with WIRED.”

While face recognition is pervasive at Dolan-owned venues, it is not universal. The iconic Chicago Theater, for example, doesn’t collect biometric data, because private entities are forbidden to do so under an Illinois law that prohibits such surveillance without written consent. In its lawsuit against WIRED, the Garden highlighted at least two statements from current and former lawmakers that discussed WIRED’s reporting along with “existing and proposed biometric-data legislation.” WIRED’s coverage was damaging to the organization, the Garden added, because the public’s reaction to it would increase “the foreseeable regulatory and political consequences to MSG.”

Attempts to rein in MSG’s face-recognition system in New York City and state long predate WIRED’s reporting. A multiyear inquiry by the state liquor authority into the Garden’s use of face recognition ended this week with a “slap on wrist,” in the words of The New York Times. A recent measure in the State Assembly targeting biometric surveillance in public spaces (like MSG) was cosponsored by Simone and Zohran Mamdani, who now serves as New York’s mayor. It’s not hard to imagine why event planners intent on privacy would want to disable the controversial MSG camera system for their ultrahigh-profile clients. “I don’t blame them for turning it off, but let’s be clear,” said Dahl. “Privacy shouldn’t just be afforded to the rich and famous.”
